We call each row of a truth table a model - it models a possible world in which each propositional letter is either true, 111, or false, 000.

Equivalence

While A∧BA \wedge BA∧B and B∧AB \wedge AB∧A are syntactically (as expressions) different, they are semantically (in meaning) the same. We call this equivalence, and use a three-line equal sign to denote this, i.e., A∧B≡B∧AA \wedge B \equiv B \wedge AA∧B≡B∧A. How do we know when two formulas φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ are equivalent? We define equivalence to mean the following. Write out the truth tables for φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ (pronounced “fye” and “sigh”), and look at the columns of both main connectives. If they are the same in every row (model), then φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ, otherwise φ≢ψ\varphi \not\equiv \psiφ​≡ψ. For example, the following truth table demonstrates that A∧B≡B∧AA \wedge B \equiv B \wedge AA∧B≡B∧A

0 1 0 0
1 0 0 0
1 1 1 1

Logical Consequence

Logical consequence is a weaker notion than logical equivalence. We say φ⊨ψ\varphi \vDash \psiφ⊨ψ, or “ψ\psiψ is a logical consequence of φ\varphiφ”, when every model (row) in which φ\varphiφ is 111, ψ\psiψ is also 111. Notice that we don’t care about models where φ\varphiφ is 000, but if ψ\psiψ is 000 at any model, φ\varphiφ must also be 000 there to ensure φ⊨ψ\varphi \vDash \psiφ⊨ψ (why couldn’t it by 111 at φ\varphiφ?). Note that having both φ⊨ψ\varphi \vDash \psiφ⊨ψ and ψ⊨φ\psi \vDash \varphiψ⊨φ tells you that φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ (think about why). Here is a demonstration of A⊨A∨BA \vDash A \vee BA⊨A∨B, in which the last 2 models are the only ones we need to check to ensure logical consequence (these are where the first formula AAA is true).

Validity and Satisfiability

We introduced 4 statements about formulas, which speak about the models of a formula. Looking at its truth table, we say a formula is

  • satisfiable - if there is at least one model that makes it true
  • valid/a tautology - if every model makes it true
  • non-valid - if there is at least one model that makes it false
  • unsatisfiable/a contradiction - if every model makes it false

Importantly - these options aren’t mutually exclusive, for example, every valid formula is satisfiable, every unsatisfiable formula is non-valid, and every formula which is neither valid, nor unsatisfiable, is both satisfiable and non-valid! Here’s an illustration of these relationships.

Despite the diagram’s proportions, the vast majority of formulas tend to be in the purple section - both satisfiable and non-valid.

Negated claims

Observe the way these claims change when we assert they are not true about a formula

φ\varphiφ is not satisfiable …means the thing same as… φ\varphiφ is unsatisfiable
φ\varphiφ is not valid …means the thing same as… φ\varphiφ is non-valid
φ\varphiφ is not non-valid …means the thing same as… φ\varphiφ is valid
φ\varphiφ is not unsatisfiable …means the thing same as… φ\varphiφ is satisfiable

In contrast, a common mistake is to confuse the statement “¬φ\neg \varphi¬φ is ____” with “φ\varphiφ is not ____”, where the blanks are replaced with one of the 4 claims about formulas. Without knowing the formula φ\varphiφ, these statements are not the same. For example ¬φ\neg \varphi¬φ is satisfiable does not tell you φ\varphiφ is unsatisfiable (although it is true if φ\varphiφ is also valid - not just satisfiable). It just tells you φ\varphiφ is non-valid, since all we know is there is one model making ¬φ\neg \varphi¬φ true. Similarly, the first row, A↦0A \mapsto 0A↦0 and B↦0B \mapsto 0B↦0 is sufficent evidence to show A∨BA \vee BA∨B is non-valid, and that A∧¬AA \wedge \neg AA∧¬A is non-valid. Of course any one row is sufficient evidence that A⇒AA \Rightarrow AA⇒A is satisfiable and that A∧¬AA \wedge \neg AA∧¬A is non-valid. When proving satisfiability or non-validity, you do not need to present the whole truth table, and it is always best to explicitly identify the model which makes the formula true/false. A bad proof will claim there is a model that makes a formula true/false, without stating a particular model.

On the other hand, validity/tautology and unsatisfiability/contradiction claims require the whole truth table…\dots… or resolution!

Resolution

CNF and RCNF

First we convert to CNF (conjunctive normal form), i.e., a formula of the form , where each blank is a literal (a propositional letter or its negation).

  1. Eliminate all occurrences of ⊕\oplus⊕, using A⊕B≡(A∨B)∧(¬A∨¬B)A \oplus B \equiv (A \vee B) \wedge (\neg A \vee \neg B)A⊕B≡(A∨B)∧(¬A∨¬B).
  2. Eliminate all occurrences of ⇔\Leftrightarrow⇔, using A⇔B≡(A⇒B)∧(B⇒A)A \Leftrightarrow B \equiv (A \Rightarrow B) \wedge (B \Rightarrow A)A⇔B≡(A⇒B)∧(B⇒A).
  3. Eliminate all occurrences of ⇒\Rightarrow⇒ using A⇒B≡¬A∨BA \Rightarrow B \equiv \neg A \vee BA⇒B≡¬A∨B.
  4. Use De Morgan’s Laws to push ¬\neg¬ inward over ∧\wedge∧ and ∨\vee∨, that is, ¬(A∧B)≡¬A∨¬B\neg (A \wedge B) \equiv \neg A \vee \neg B¬(A∧B)≡¬A∨¬B and ¬(A∨B)≡¬A∧¬B\neg (A \vee B) \equiv \neg A \wedge \neg B¬(A∨B)≡¬A∧¬B
  5. Eliminate double negations using ¬¬A≡A\neg\neg A \equiv A¬¬A≡A.
  6. Use the distributive laws to get the required form; you should need only A∨(B∧C)≡(A∨B)∧(A∨C)A \vee (B \wedge C) \equiv (A \vee B) \wedge (A \vee C)A∨(B∧C)≡(A∨B)∧(A∨C). You will likely need to apply this repeatedly, and any of A,B,CA,B,CA,B,C could be a larger complex formula.

To convert to RCNF (reduced CNF), delete any repetitions of literals, for example (A∨B∨A)≡(A∨B)(A \vee B \vee A) \equiv (A \vee B)(A∨B∨A)≡(A∨B), and drop any clause which contains a formula and its negation, like (A∨B∨¬A)(A \vee B \vee \neg A)(A∨B∨¬A) (these are always true, so can be ignored).

Finally we can convert RCNF to Clausal Form - a set of clauses, where each clause is a set of literals.

(P∨¬Q∨S)∧(P∨¬R∨S)∧(¬S∨¬P)∧(¬S∨Q∨R)(P \vee \neg Q \vee S) \wedge (P \vee \neg R \vee S) \wedge (\neg S \vee \neg P) \wedge (\neg S \vee Q \vee R)(P∨¬Q∨S)∧(P∨¬R∨S)∧(¬S∨¬P)∧(¬S∨Q∨R) {{P,¬Q,S},{P,¬R,S},{¬S,¬P},{¬S,Q,R}} \{\{P, \neg Q, S\}, \{P, \neg R, S\}, \{\neg S, \neg P\}, \{\neg S, Q, R\}\}{{P,¬Q,S},{P,¬R,S},{¬S,¬P},{¬S,Q,R}}

Resolving Clauses

If we can find a propositional letter AAA and two clauses, X,YX, YX,Y with P∈XP \in XP∈X and ¬P∈Y\neg P \in Y¬P∈Y (like {P,A,B}\{P,A,B\}{P,A,B} and {¬P,C,D,E}\{\neg P, C, D, E\}{¬P,C,D,E}) we can conclude the clause (X∪Y)∖{P,¬P}(X \cup Y) \setminus \{P,\neg P\}(X∪Y)∖{P,¬P}, that is, all the literals in either X or Y that aren’t PPP or ¬P\neg P¬P, as a logical consequence. For example, the clauses {P,A,B}\{P,A,B\}{P,A,B} and {¬P,C,D,E}\{\neg P, C, D, E\}{¬P,C,D,E} resolve to {A,B,C,D,E}\{A,B,C,D,E\}{A,B,C,D,E}. Paying attention to the precise definition of logical consequence, pick two small clauses that can be resolved, and convince yourself that the resolvent is a logical consequence of those two clauses together. Importantly, the resolvent is not equivalent to the previous two, it’s just a logical consequence.

In the above image, we make use of the principle written in red, which says that if we can ever derive a contradiction (an unsatisfiable clause), we can conclude the unsatisfiability of the original clauses. We consider the empty clause, written ∅\emptyset∅, unsatisfiable, because the only way to derive it is to resolve a pair of clauses of the form {P}\{P\}{P} and {¬P}\{\neg P\}{¬P}, which together represent P∧¬PP \wedge \neg PP∧¬P (clearly unsatisfiable).

A common misconception

What do the clauses {A,B}\{A,B\}{A,B} and {¬A,¬B}\{\neg A, \neg B\}{¬A,¬B} resolve to? Is it ∅\emptyset∅? No! That would mean that (A∨B)∧(¬A∨¬B)(A \vee B) \wedge (\neg A \vee \neg B)(A∨B)∧(¬A∨¬B) is unsatisfiable, but it isn’t! Consider the model where AAA is true and BBB is false - this makes the formula true.

These clauses actually resolve to either {B,¬B}\{B,\neg B\}{B,¬B} or to {A,¬A}\{A,\neg A\}{A,¬A}, since we can only resolve on one letter at a time. Both true of these formulas are always in any model - so are equivalent to t\textbf{t}t (true). But having “true” as a logical consequence tells you nothing! All formulas have “true” as a logical consequence. Given only these clauses, we are at a dead end - there is no contradiction among them.

Usage of Resolution

ONLY USE RESOLUTION ON A FORMULA TO SHOW IT IS UNSATISFIABLE.

Resolution doesn’t tell you “whether or not” your formula is unsatisfiable - in its basic form, it can only confirm that a formula is unsatisfiable. It is true that a set of clauses representing a satisfiable (i.e. not unsatisfiable) formula cannot derive the empty set via resolution, but to show satisfiability of the formula in this way, you’d need to demonstrate that your algorithm for choosing clauses to resolve will always derive the empty set if its possible, and that your algorithm fails to do so for the formula in question. There are better ways!

I want to show my formula is…

  • unsatisfiable/a contradiction - convert to RCNF and resolve to ∅\emptyset∅
  • valid/a tautology - negate the formula, then convert to RCNF and resolve to ∅\emptyset∅
  • satisfiable - find a model which makes the formula true
  • non-valid - find a model which makes the formula false

The first two of these demonstrate a fact about every model in a formula’s truth table via resolution, and the second two skip resolution and just demonstrate that at least one model makes the formula true/false - much easier! Note that the first two are sometimes quicker demonstrated by writing down the truth table - resolution comes in handy when the truth table grows too large.

Showing logical consequence and equivalence

Given formulas φ\varphiφ and ψ\psiψ, to show…

  • φ⊨ψ\varphi \vDash \psiφ⊨ψ - construct φ∧¬ψ\varphi \wedge \neg \psiφ∧¬ψ, convert it to RCNF, then resolve to ∅\emptyset∅. Note, this is just showing that φ⇒ψ\varphi \Rightarrow \psiφ⇒ψ is valid, since ¬(φ⇒ψ)≡φ∧¬ψ\neg(\varphi \Rightarrow \psi) \equiv \varphi \wedge \neg \psi¬(φ⇒ψ)≡φ∧¬ψ.
  • φ≡ψ\varphi \equiv \psiφ≡ψ - construct φ⊕ψ\varphi \oplus \psiφ⊕ψ, convert to RCNF, then resolve to ∅\emptyset∅.

More generally, we can demonstrate that a conclusion ψ\psiψ follows from the assumption of premises φ1,φ2,…φn\varphi_1, \varphi_2, \dots \varphi_nφ1​,φ2​,…φn​, we consider the formula φ1∧φ2∧…φn∧¬ψ\varphi_1 \wedge \varphi_2 \wedge \dots \varphi_n \wedge \neg \psiφ1​∧φ2​∧…φn​∧¬ψ, convert it to RCNF, and resolve to ∅\emptyset∅. Again, this is just demonstrating that (φ1∧φ2∧⋯∧φn)⇒ψ(\varphi_1 \wedge \varphi_2 \wedge \dots \wedge \varphi_n) \Rightarrow \psi(φ1​∧φ2​∧⋯∧φn​)⇒ψ is valid (a tautology). Note that each premise, φi\varphi_iφi​, can be separately converted to clausal form and combined with the rest, since they are all part of a larger conjunction (remember to negate the conclusion, ψ\psiψ, before converting to RCNF).
